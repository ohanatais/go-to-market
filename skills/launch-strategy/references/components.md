# Component Deep-Dives

The per-phase method. Load when a session reaches the depth of a phase. Examples use three
*contrasting* products so the method is visible without implying one launch shape is correct:

- **Product A — a developer tool** (users in technical communities; values proof and craft).
- **Product B — a local consumer app** (users found regionally, by word of mouth).
- **Product C — a B2B workflow tool** (users reached by direct outreach and partnerships).

The user's product is its own case — derive its launch, don't copy these.

## 1. Foundation

**Goal:** know exactly who you're launching to and where they already are.

**Method:**
1. Pull positioning: who it's for and the onliness line ("the only ___ that ___"). If missing,
   derive a working version — you can't aim a launch at "everyone."
2. The launch-specific addition: **map where these specific people already gather.** Not
   demographics — actual places. Which communities (subreddits, Discords, Slack groups, forums),
   which platforms, which newsletters, which search terms, which events. Be concrete; "on social
   media" is not a place.
3. Note where you can *contribute before you promote* — the places where showing up as a
   helpful member first earns the right to launch later.

| | Product A (dev tool) | Product B (local consumer) | Product C (B2B) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Where they gather | Hacker News, dev subreddits, niche Discords | regional FB groups, local press, word of mouth | LinkedIn, industry Slacks, partner networks |
| Contribute-first move | answer technical questions, open-source a piece | be genuinely useful in local groups | share insight in industry communities |

## 2. Channels (Bullseye)

**Goal:** find the one channel that actually works, instead of guessing.

**Method (the three rings — see `core-principles.md`):**
1. **Brainstorm (outer):** for each channel *type*, write one concrete tactic for this product.
   Force breadth — the channel that works is often not the obvious one.
2. **Test cheaply (middle):** pick the 2–3 most promising, run small experiments, watch the
   metric. The question is "signs of life?", not "full commitment."
3. **Focus (inner):** the channel that moves the metric gets the effort; stop dabbling in the
   rest.

Tie every channel to the **metric that proves it** (sign-ups, returning users), not to activity
(posts published). A channel that generates attention but no returning users has failed the
test.

| | Product A | Product B | Product C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Likely winning channel | content + community (Show HN, technical posts) | local word of mouth + community | direct outreach + partnerships |
| A cheap test | one strong technical post in one community | seed 3 local groups, measure signups | 20 personal outreach messages, measure replies |

The method is identical; the winning channel differs entirely by product. That's the point —
don't inherit someone else's channel.

## 3. Pre-launch

**Goal:** arrive at launch day with a warm audience instead of a cold start.

**Method:**
1. **Waitlist.** Stand up a simple landing page with a clear promise and capture emails before
   the product exists. This both warms an audience and tests demand (and gives you people to
   talk to — hand to `/first-users` for the conversations). Give people a reason to join and a
   reason to share.
2. **Build-in-public.** Turn the building process into content: decisions, progress, failures,
   the why behind the product. Done consistently over weeks, this builds an invested audience
   that shows up and amplifies on launch day. It's infrastructure, not vanity — frame each post
   as warming the launch, not performing.
3. **Timeline.** Work backward from launch day: what gets built and shared each week so the
   audience is warm and the assets are ready when the day arrives.

**Illustrative pre-launch arcs:**
- Product A: build-in-public on the technical craft; open-source a useful piece to seed
  credibility; waitlist for early access.
- Product B: be useful in local communities for weeks; collect a waitlist of locals who want it.
- Product C: publish insight that attracts the target role; warm a list of named prospects via
  direct relationship.

## 4. Launch day

**Goal:** a coordinated, sequenced push — not a frantic scramble.

**Method — a sequenced checklist with timing:**
1. **Notify the warm audience first** — the waitlist and build-in-public following hear before
   the world; they're your initial momentum and social proof.
2. **Publish the assets** — landing page live, launch post(s) ready (write them with
   `/product-copy`), demo working.
3. **Post to the chosen channels in order** — staggered, not all at once, so you can engage each.
4. **Engage all day** — reply, thank, answer. Launch day is a conversation, not a broadcast.
5. **Amplifiers if they fit** — Product Hunt, Show HN, a press angle. These reward preparation
   and an existing base; if you have neither, they'll underwhelm. Decide deliberately whether
   each fits this product rather than defaulting to it.

A launch-day checklist should have an order, rough times, and (even solo) clear "what happens
when." The calm of a sequenced plan is itself part of why it works.

## 5. Post-launch & measurement

**Goal:** learn whether it worked, and what to do next.

**Method:**
1. **Measure against real goals**, set before launch: how many real sign-ups, how many *came
   back* (the retention question), waitlist conversion, which channel drove the most returning
   users. Separate vanity (spike, likes) from traction (returning users).
2. **Read the channel signal** — which channel actually produced returning users? That's your
   inner-ring channel to double down on.
3. **Sustain** — launch day is the start. Keep engaging the new users (hand to `/first-users`
   for turning them into the engine for the next cohort), keep publishing, keep the momentum.
4. **Decide the next move** — optimize the working channel, fix what the launch revealed, and
   plan the *next* launch (a new milestone, audience, or channel). Relaunches compound.

**What "it worked" looks like (honest version):** not a viral moment, but a real channel
identified and a first cohort of users who stay. If the launch was quiet but you learned your
channel and got returning users, it worked. If it spiked and everyone left, it didn't — and the
fix is usually upstream (positioning, product, or the wrong audience), not a louder launch.
