# Core Principles

Loaded for every launch-strategy session. The evidence base and the stances that turn a launch
from a hopeful day into a campaign that produces real, returning users.

## A launch is a campaign, not a moment

The default mental model — "launch = the day we go live" — is the root of most failed launches.
A launch is a campaign with a before, a during, and an after, and the during is the smallest
part. Reframe the user away from "what do I do on launch day?" toward "what am I doing in the
weeks before, so launch day lands somewhere warm?" The launch day everyone admires is the
visible tip of quiet pre-launch work.

## The 50% rule

From Weinberg & Mares (*Traction*): spend roughly half your effort on building the product and
half on getting traction — and start the traction half early, not after the product is "done."
The most common solo-builder failure is 100% product, 0% distribution, arriving at a finished
product with no audience and no channel. Traction work is not a reward you earn after building;
it runs in parallel from early on.

## Traction is evidence of demand, not applause

Traction is measurable evidence that people want this: sign-ups, active users, sales,
retention. Likes, congratulations from peers, and a spike of launch-day traffic that never
returns are *not* traction — they're vanity that feels like progress. Every launch goal should
be stated in real terms (this many real users who came back, this many on the waitlist who
converted), and every launch should be measured against that, honestly. A launch that produced
a traffic spike and no returning users did not work, no matter how good the day felt.

## The Bullseye Framework — find the one channel

Most growth, at any given time, comes from *one* channel done well — and the hard part is
finding which one. Guessing and pouring everything into a single channel is how founders waste
months. The Bullseye process (from *Traction*):

1. **Outer ring — brainstorm.** Consider the full range of channel types (content, SEO, ads,
   community, PR, partnerships, direct sales, existing platforms, speaking, viral, and more).
   For each, imagine at least one concrete tactic for *this* product. The point is to not
   prematurely rule channels out.
2. **Middle ring — test cheaply.** Pick the few most promising and run small, cheap experiments
   — not full commitments. The question is "does this channel show signs of working for us?"
3. **Inner ring — focus.** Move the one channel that actually moves the metric to the center,
   and pour effort into optimizing it. Stop spreading thin.

The discipline is twofold: explore widely (don't assume you know your channel), then commit
narrowly (don't keep dabbling once one works).

## Pre-launch is where launches are won

The phase founders skip — and the one that decides the outcome. Two pieces:

- **The waitlist.** Capture interest *before* there's a product to show. A waitlist is both a
  warm audience for launch day and a pre-launch signal of demand (and a place to talk to future
  users). You can build a waitlist with nothing but a landing page and a clear promise.
- **Build-in-public as infrastructure.** Sharing the building process publicly is not just
  personal branding — it's pre-launch infrastructure. Each post builds an audience that is
  already warm, already invested in the story, and primed to show up and share on launch day.
  Done over weeks, it turns launch day from a cold start into a reunion.

If a user is reading this and hasn't started pre-launch, the advice is: start it now, in
parallel with building, even if launch is months away.

## Launch day is sequenced, not frantic

Launch day is a coordinated push, not a scramble — a checklist with an order and timing: notify
the waitlist, publish the assets, post to the chosen channels in sequence, engage all day, and
(if it fits) run the Product Hunt or similar. Amplifiers like Product Hunt only pay off with
preparation and an existing base; defaulting to them without either is a common waste. Decide
whether each amplifier fits this product rather than treating it as a mandatory ritual.

## Relaunches are underrated

A single launch rarely makes a product. The honest expectation for a first launch is a quiet
one that yields *evidence* and a *channel insight*, plus the first cohort of real users. That's
a win. Then launch again — to a new channel, a new audience, a new milestone. Founders treat
launch as a one-shot event; the ones who get traction relaunch repeatedly.

## Universal — derive from this market

Where users gather, what reads as spam vs. welcome, which platforms matter, and what a launch
even looks like are all specific to the product, the persona, the country, and the language. A
generic "Product Hunt + LinkedIn" plan is a tell that the launch wasn't derived from where the
real users are. Always anchor the channels and the plan to *this* product's actual audience and
market.
