# First Users

The first users are the hardest. You have no social proof, no reviews, no word of mouth — you
have to find each person and give them a reason to care. So most builders do the wrong thing:
they "launch" to a passive crowd and wait to be discovered. The crowd doesn't come.

The first users are not *attracted*; they're *found*. This skill helps a solo builder get the
first real, returning users the way nearly every successful startup actually did it — by hand,
one person at a time, doing things that don't scale.

## Language rule

**Always respond in the user's language.** If the user writes in Portuguese (including
Brazilian Portuguese), do this work in Portuguese — the outreach scripts, the questions to ask,
the examples. You'll be drafting messages the user actually sends and questions they actually
ask real people; those have to be native to the user's market and language, not translated.

## The mindset shift that makes this possible

Three ideas reframe "I need users" from overwhelming to doable:

- **Do things that don't scale (Paul Graham).** The most common unscalable thing founders must
  do at the start is recruit users manually — and give them an overwhelmingly good experience.
  Airbnb went door to door; Stripe onboarded users by hand. This feels inefficient and is
  exactly right: at the start, manual *is* the strategy.
- **You need 10, then 1000 — not a million (Kevin Kelly).** You don't need to go viral. You
  need a small number of true users who genuinely want this, with whom you build a direct
  relationship. Starting from zero, the first target is **10 real users**, not a crowd. That's
  findable by hand this week.
- **Talk about them, not you (The Mom Test).** When you talk to potential users, the goal is to
  learn about *their* life and problem, not to pitch yours. People are polite about ideas and
  honest about their lives — so ask what they *did*, not what they *think* of your product.

Together: find a handful of specific people who have the problem now, talk to them about the
problem, give the ones who try it an unreasonably good experience, and turn them into the
engine for the next handful.

## The model: five layers, in order

```
1. Reframe          →  10 true users found by hand — not a crowd attracted
2. Find them        →  Identify specific people who have the problem right now, where they are
3. The conversation →  Talk about their problem, not your pitch (The Mom Test)
4. Onboard by hand  →  An overwhelmingly good first experience; do the unscalable things
5. Turn into engine →  Retention (stay vs. churn), referrals, the next cohort
```

Why the order: you can't reach out before you know *who* (find), the conversation only works if
you're hunting the problem not pitching (talk), activation is what makes a found user a real one
(onboard), and the first users only compound if they stay and bring others (engine). Skipping to
"how do I get a thousand users" without the first ten by hand is the classic mistake.

## Entry point: where is the user?

**Signal 1 — how many users so far?** Zero (start at find + reach), a few but stuck (the
conversation or activation), or some who don't stay (retention/engine).

**Signal 2 — do they know who has the problem?** A persona, a community, named individuals? Reuse
it. If it's vague, the first work is getting specific — you can't find "everyone."

**Signal 3 — built and usable?** This skill assumes a usable product (even rough). If they're
pre-build and testing an idea, that's `/personas` / idea validation, not this.

## How each layer works (the short version)

**1. Reframe.** Set the real first target: **10 true users**, found by hand, this week or this
month. Kill the "I need to go viral" framing — it's the reason nothing happens. Small and manual
is the path, not a compromise.

**2. Find them.** Identify *specific people who have the problem right now* — not a demographic.
Where do they already gather (communities, threads, events), and who has visibly expressed this
pain recently (a complaint, a workaround, a question)? Make a concrete list of reachable humans.

**3. The conversation.** Reach out to learn, not to sell (The Mom Test). Ask about how they
handle the problem today, the last time it bit them, what they've tried. Avoid "would you use
X?" — it produces polite lies. The conversation that converts is one where they feel understood;
the offer comes after, almost as an afterthought.

**4. Onboard by hand.** For the first users, do the unscalable: set them up personally, hold
their hand, fix their problem yourself if you must. The goal is an *overwhelmingly good* first
experience — better than the product can deliver at scale yet — because these few people are how
you learn what "great" means and earn your first advocates.

**5. Turn into engine.** A found user only matters if they *stay*. Watch the gap between users
who return after the first session and those who vanish, and learn why. Ask happy users for the
one specific introduction. The first cohort, treated unreasonably well, becomes the source of
the next — that's how 10 becomes 50 becomes 100.

## Universal by design — fit this product's people

The frameworks are fixed; the tactics are not. Where the first users are, how you reach them
without being spam, and what an "overwhelmingly good experience" means differ completely for a
developer tool, a local service, and a B2B product. Derive the *who* and the *how* from this
product's actual users, market, and language — never from a generic "post in some Facebook
groups" playbook. If the plan stops naming *specific* people and places, re-derive from the
persona.

## Output always includes

1. **The target** — the real first goal (usually 10 true users) and the reframe away from
   crowds.
2. **A find list** — concrete: specific people, communities, and recent signals of the problem,
   that the user can actually reach this week.
3. **The conversation approach** — Mom-Test questions and an outreach script that learns rather
   than pitches, in the user's language.
4. **A by-hand onboarding plan** — how to give the first users an overwhelmingly good first
   experience.
5. **The engine** — what to watch for retention (stay vs. churn) and how to ask for the next
   introduction.
6. **Next step** — usually `/launch-strategy` for scaling the channel once one works, and
   `/product-copy` for the messages and landing.

## The early-stage truth

Getting the first users is unglamorous, manual, and slow — and that's not a sign you're doing it
wrong. It's the work. Ten real users who came back and one who told a friend is worth more than
a thousand visitors who bounced. Do the unscalable things now; you'll have the rest of the
product's life to automate them.
