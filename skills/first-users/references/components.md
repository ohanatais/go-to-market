# Component Deep-Dives

The per-layer method, with the actual questions and scripts. Load when a session reaches the
depth of a layer. Examples use three *contrasting* products so the method is visible without
implying one tactic is correct:

- **Product A — a developer tool** (first users in technical communities).
- **Product B — a local service app** (first users found regionally, in person and in local groups).
- **Product C — a B2B niche tool** (first users reached by direct, named outreach).

Derive the user's tactics from their product; don't copy these.

## 1. Reframe

**Goal:** replace "I need lots of users" with a concrete, small, manual target.

**Method:** set the first target at **10 true users** found by hand, with a short time box (this
week / this month). Name out loud what you're *not* doing yet: going viral, running ads, building
an audience. The reframe is the unlock — it turns a hopeless ocean into a findable list. Revisit
it whenever the user drifts back toward "but how do I get thousands."

---

## 2. Find them

**Goal:** a concrete list of specific, reachable people who have the problem now.

**Method:**
1. Start from the persona (`/personas` if it exists). Translate it from a demographic into
   **places and signals**: where do these people already gather, and where have they *recently
   shown the problem* — a complaint, a question, a workaround, a "does anyone know how to…"?
2. Build an actual list: named individuals, specific threads, specific communities. Aim for
   reachable humans, not categories.
3. Prefer people showing the problem *right now* — recency means the pain is live and the person
   is receptive.

| | Product A (dev tool) | Product B (local service) | Product C (B2B niche) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Where they gather | dev subreddits, niche Discords, GitHub issues | local FB groups, neighborhood apps, in person | LinkedIn, industry Slacks, conference lists |
| A recent signal to look for | "anyone solved X?" threads | "can someone recommend…" posts | a role posting about the exact pain |
| The find list | 15 people who hit this in the last month | 10 locals who asked for this | 20 named people in the target role |

## 3. The conversation

**Goal:** reach out in a way that learns and earns trust — and converts *because* it doesn't pitch.

**Method (The Mom Test applied to outreach):**
1. **Open about them, not you.** Reference the specific thing they said or did. You're reaching
   out because you saw *their* problem, not to announce *your* product.
2. **Ask about their life and their past:**
   - "How do you handle [problem] today? Walk me through the last time it came up."
   - "What have you tried? What annoyed you about it?"
   - "How much of a headache is this, honestly — or is it fine as is?"
   - Avoid: "Would you use a tool that…?" / "Do you think this is a good idea?" (polite lies).
3. **Let the offer come last, small.** Once you genuinely understand their problem and it's real,
   the offer is almost an afterthought: "I'm building exactly this — want me to set you up with
   it and see if it helps?" A person who felt understood says yes far more often than one who was
   pitched.

**An outreach opener shape (adapt to the product and language):**
> "Hey [name] — saw your [specific post/comment] about [specific problem]. I keep running into
> people dealing with that. Mind if I ask how you handle it today? (Not selling anything — I'm
> building in this space and trying to understand it properly.)"

The "not selling anything" is true at this point: the first message learns. The offer comes only
after the problem is confirmed real.

## 4. Onboard by hand

**Goal:** give the first users an overwhelmingly good first experience — by doing the unscalable.

**Method:**
1. **Set them up personally.** Don't send a link and hope. Walk them through it, do the setup for
   them, get them to their first real value with you present. (Stripe-style hand installation;
   Airbnb-style personal help.)
2. **Over-deliver beyond what scales.** Fix their specific issue yourself, add the thing they
   asked for, follow up personally. These few people are worth unreasonable effort — they teach
   you what "great" is and become advocates.
3. **Watch the first session closely.** Where do they hesitate, get confused, light up? This is
   the richest product feedback you'll ever get; it's also activation in real time.

| | Product A | Product B | Product C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unscalable onboarding | pair with them to integrate it; fix their edge case live | do the first job *for* them | a personal setup call; configure it for their case |

## 5. Turn into engine

**Goal:** make found users *stay*, and become the source of the next cohort.

**Method:**
1. **Watch stay vs. churn.** Track the simple, brutal metric: did they come back after the first
   session? For those who didn't, find out *why* (ask — a churned early user will often tell you).
   Fixing the reason they left matters more than adding new sign-ups onto a leaky experience.
2. **Ask for the specific introduction.** From a happy user, don't say "tell your friends" — ask
   for *one* named person: "Who's the one person you know who has this exact problem? Mind
   introducing us?" Specific asks get acted on; vague ones don't.
3. **Let the cohort compound.** Each happy, retained user is a source of the next few — through
   intros, vouching, and word of mouth in their community. Repeat the loop: a few users made
   genuinely happy → learn → the next few largely through them. That's how 10 becomes 50 becomes
   100, before any channel scales.

**When to hand off:** once a *pattern* emerges — a community or move that reliably produces
returning users — that's a channel worth scaling. Hand to `/launch-strategy` (Bullseye) to test
and focus it. The manual work here finds the channel; launch-strategy scales it.
