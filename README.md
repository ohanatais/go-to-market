# go-to-market

> Shipping is not launching. Launching is the work that happens around the ship.

Part of the [build-from-zero](https://github.com/ohanatais/build-from-zero) ecosystem.

Most solo builders treat launch as a moment: the day the product goes live. Then they post on LinkedIn, get a hundred likes from friends and colleagues who will never become users, and wonder why nothing happened.

Launch is a campaign and, like any campaign, it requires strategy, sequencing, and an honest understanding of where your first real users actually are — and what it takes to reach them.

This repo covers everything between "the product is ready" and "I have users who stay."

---

## What's inside

### Launch strategy
How to plan a launch that generates real traction — not just a spike on day one.

A launch without an audience is a product falling in a forest. This guide is about building the conditions for launch before launch day arrives, so that when you go live, you're releasing something into a space that's already been warmed up.

- The three phases of a launch: pre-launch, launch day, and post-launch — what happens in each and why skipping pre-launch is the most common mistake
- How to build a waitlist before you have a product to show
- How to use build-in-public content as pre-launch infrastructure, not just personal branding
- Launch day checklist: what to do, in what order, and what not to do
- How to measure whether a launch worked — and what to do when it doesn't

### Channels
Where your first users actually are — and how to reach them without sounding like spam.

"Post on social media" is not a channel strategy. Every product has a specific persona, and every persona has specific places they trust, specific communities they participate in, and specific reasons they pay attention to new things. Generic outreach gets ignored. Specific, contextual outreach gets users.

- How to map the channels where your persona actually spends time
- Community-based distribution: Reddit, Discord, Facebook groups, Slack communities — how to contribute before you promote
- Product Hunt: when it makes sense, how to prepare, and what to realistically expect
- LinkedIn: how to use it without becoming someone who only posts about their own product
- Cold outreach: the only version that works (and why most people do it wrong)
- SEO as a slow but compounding long-term channel

### First users
How to get your first 10, 50, and 100 real users — without ads and without an existing audience.

The first users are the hardest. You have no social proof, no reviews, no word of mouth. You have to find each person manually and give them a reason to care. This guide is about doing that systematically rather than randomly.

- Why your first 10 users should be found, not attracted
- How to identify specific people who have the problem you solve — right now
- The conversation that converts: how to talk about your product without pitching it
- What to ask your first users that no survey will ever tell you
- How to turn early users into the engine for the next 100
- The difference between users who stay and users who churn after the first session — and what to do about it

---

## A note on timing

This repo is most useful when you're about to launch or actively preparing for it. But the thinking here should start earlier than you'd expect.

The best launches are built over weeks or months before the product is ready — through content, community participation, and relationship-building that happens in the background while you're still building. If you're reading this and you haven't started yet: start the pre-launch work now, in parallel with building.

**Claude Code skills** for each stage live in [pm-skills](https://github.com/ohanatais/pm-skills) — specifically `estrategia-de-lancamento` and `primeiros-usuarios`.
