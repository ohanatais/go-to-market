# Core Principles

Loaded for every first-users session. The evidence base and the stances that turn "I need users"
into a concrete, doable set of manual moves.

## Found, not attracted

The instinct from zero is to build something that *attracts* users — a clever launch, a viral
post, an ad. From zero, that almost never works: you have no proof, no audience, no momentum to
amplify. The first users are *found*, one at a time, by reaching out to specific people. This is
the single most important reframe in the skill, because it replaces a passive, hopeless posture
("how do I get discovered?") with an active, doable one ("who are ten specific people I can reach
this week?").

## Do things that don't scale (Paul Graham)

The most common unscalable thing founders must do at the start is recruit users manually — and
it's not a phase to rush past, it's the strategy. Two dimensions:

- **Recruit manually.** Go to where the users are and bring them in by hand. Airbnb went door to
  door in New York; Stripe ("Collison installation") onboarded users by literally setting it up
  for them on the spot. Manual recruiting feels too slow to matter — and it's how most successful
  startups got their first users.
- **Give an overwhelmingly good experience.** Deliver the first users something better than the
  product can sustain at scale — hand-hold them, fix their issue personally, over-deliver. A
  small number of users made *extremely* happy is worth more than a large number left lukewarm:
  they teach you what great means and become your first advocates.

The fear "but this doesn't scale" is the point. You do the unscalable things *now*, while there
are few enough users that you can; you automate later, once you know what's worth automating.

## You need 10, then 1000 — not a million (Kevin Kelly)

Kelly's "1000 true fans" reframes the goal: you don't need mass-market reach to have a real
thing — you need a manageable number of *true* users who genuinely want this and with whom you
build a direct relationship. For a builder at zero, the operative target is even smaller: **the
first 10 real, returning users.** Ten is findable by hand this week; "a thousand" or "go viral"
is paralysis. Hit 10, learn, then 50, then 100 — each cohort fed largely by the last.

## Talk about them, not you (The Mom Test)

When you talk to potential users, the goal is to learn the truth about their life and problem —
and people are polite about *ideas* but honest about their *lives and past behavior*. Rob
Fitzpatrick's rules:

- **Ask about their life, not your idea.** "How do you handle this today? Walk me through the
  last time it happened." Not "would you use an app that does X?"
- **Ask about the past, not the future.** What people *did* is data; what they say they *would*
  do is a polite guess. "What did you do about it?" beats "would you pay for this?"
- **Stop pitching; start listening.** The moment you pitch, they switch to being nice. The most
  useful conversations barely mention your product until the end.

This is the same discipline used in idea validation (`/personas`), now aimed at acquisition: the
conversation that *learns* is also the conversation that *converts*, because a person who feels
genuinely understood is far more likely to try what you offer.

## Specificity is everything

"My users are small business owners" is not a find list; "the three people who complained about
this exact problem in that subreddit last week" is. The work of finding first users is the work
of getting *specific* — specific people, specific places, specific recent signals of the problem.
Every time the plan drifts back to a demographic or "social media," push it back to named humans
and named places. Vague targeting is why outreach fails.

## Activation, then retention, is the real result

A sign-up is not a user. The result that matters is someone who came back after the first session
— activation and retention. So watch the gap between users who return and users who vanish, and
treat *learning why they churn* as more valuable than adding more sign-ups on top of a leaky
experience. Ten users who stay beats a hundred who bounce; fixing the bounce comes before scaling
the top.

## The first cohort is the engine

Treated unreasonably well, the first users become the source of the next: they refer specific
people, they vouch, they tell you what to fix. So the loop isn't "get users" once — it's "make a
few users genuinely happy, learn from them, get the next few largely through them," repeated.
Asking a happy user for *one specific introduction* (not "tell your friends") is one of the
highest-yield moves available at this stage.

## Universal — derive from this product's people

Where the first users are, what reads as spam vs. welcome, and what "overwhelmingly good"
requires are all specific to the product, the persona, the market, and the language. A generic
playbook ("post in Facebook groups, do cold email") is a sign the plan wasn't derived from who the
real users are. Always anchor to *this* product's specific people and places.
