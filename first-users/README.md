# first-users

> The first users are not attracted — they're found. One person at a time, by hand, doing the things that don't scale. It's unglamorous, and it's the work.

Part of the [go-to-market](https://github.com/ohanatais/go-to-market) skill set, within the [build-from-zero](https://github.com/ohanatais/build-from-zero) ecosystem.

---

## What this skill does

Gets a product's first 10, 50, and 100 real users — by hand, without ads or an existing audience. It covers finding specific people who have the problem right now, the conversation that converts *without* pitching, onboarding the first users personally into an overwhelmingly good experience, and turning early users into the engine for the next cohort.

It's grounded in early-traction evidence rather than growth-hack folklore: Paul Graham's "do things that don't scale," Kevin Kelly's "1000 true fans," and Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test."

The skill is **universal by design**: where the first users are, how you reach them without being spam, and what "overwhelmingly good" means differ completely for a dev tool, a local service, and a B2B product. The *who* and the *how* are derived from this product's actual people — never a generic "post in some Facebook groups" playbook.

---

## The mindset shift

```
Found, not attracted        →  reach out to specific people; don't wait to be discovered
10, not a million           →  the first target is 10 true users, findable by hand this week
Do things that don't scale  →  recruit manually, give an overwhelmingly good experience
Talk about them, not you    →  ask what they did, not what they'd think of your idea
```

---

## The model: five layers, in order

```
1. Reframe          →  10 true users found by hand — not a crowd attracted
2. Find them        →  Specific people who have the problem right now, where they are
3. The conversation →  Talk about their problem, not your pitch (The Mom Test)
4. Onboard by hand  →  An overwhelmingly good first experience; the unscalable things
5. Turn into engine →  Retention (stay vs. churn), referrals, the next cohort
```

---

## Three modes, one interface

| Mode | When to use | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **guided** | Getting first users from zero, first time | Walk the five layers, explaining the why |
| **outreach** | Knows roughly who; wants the plan + scripts | Find list + ready-to-send outreach + questions |
| **diagnose** | Has early users who aren't converting or staying | Where the funnel leaks + the one fix that matters |

---

## What every output includes

- **The target** — the real first goal (usually 10 true users) and the reframe away from crowds
- **A find list** — specific people, communities, and recent signals, reachable this week
- **The conversation approach** — Mom-Test questions + an outreach script that learns, not pitches
- **A by-hand onboarding plan** — an overwhelmingly good first experience
- **The engine** — what to watch for retention, and how to ask for the next introduction
- **Next step** — usually `/launch-strategy` and `/product-copy`

It also triggers on natural phrasing — "how do I get my first users", "find my first 10 users", "nobody's signing up", "how do I talk to users without sounding salesy", "my early users aren't sticking" — even without naming the skill.

## The relationship to the rest

First-users is the *manual, unscalable* work of getting and keeping the earliest users. Its companion [`launch-strategy`](../launch-strategy) plans the *campaign and channels* — and once your by-hand work reveals a channel that reliably produces returning users, launch-strategy scales it. Write the outreach and landing with [`product-copy`](https://github.com/ohanatais/brand-and-copy).
